CiteNexus makes one promise: **no ungrounded claim.** Not "zero hallucination" —
we won't pretend the model never errs. We guarantee it never *asserts what the
evidence doesn't support*. Three steps stand between a question and an answer you
can defend.

## Ground → verify → cite or abstain

1. **Ground.** The answer is generated at temperature 0 from retrieved passages
   only, quoted verbatim, in the question's own language.
2. **Verify.** A per-claim grounding gate checks every word of the answer against
   its cited passage. Anything unsupported is dropped before it reaches you.
3. **Cite or abstain.** A surviving claim resolves to a document, page, and bbox.
   If nothing supports the question, CiteNexus emits the single **pinned refusal
   string** — identical in every port — rather than guessing.

<Aside type="note" title="The pinned refusal">
Every port emits the exact same sentence when it abstains:
*"I can't answer that from the available evidence."* It is a fixed constant, not
a generated apology — so a refusal is always recognizable and never a stealth
hallucination.
</Aside>

## The core is byte-for-byte identical

The cite-or-abstain flow isn't re-implemented per language and hoped to match —
it's pinned by a shared [`conformance/`](https://github.com/muthuishere/citenexus/tree/main/conformance)
suite. The same corpus and questions produce the same decision, answer, and
cited passage in Go, JavaScript, and Python. Here is that flow, over the
conformance corpus:

<TabItem label="Go">
```go
corpus := []answer.Doc{
	{DocumentID: "nda", Text: "The employee shall not disclose confidential information."},
	{DocumentID: "leave", Text: "Employees are entitled to thirty days of annual leave."},
	{DocumentID: "termination", Text: "The contract termination clause requires ninety days written notice."},
}

// Grounded → answered, cited to "nda".
answered := answer.Ask(corpus, "Can the employee disclose confidential information?", answer.DefaultTopK)

// Unsupported → the pinned refusal.
refused := answer.Ask(corpus, "What is the capital of France?", answer.DefaultTopK)
```

## Navigate, don't cite

Higher-level structure — graph and wiki — is **navigation over evidence**, never
a citation itself. Every hit resolves *down* to a bbox- or `file:line`-cited
Evidence Unit before an answer is generated. You can browse the map, but the
answer is always anchored to a real passage.
